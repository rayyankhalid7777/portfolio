Acoustic Wave Spatiotemporal Analysis

2024 | UC San Diego - MAE 170

Lab Report

In this experiment, we aimed to analyze the spatiotemporal propagation of a sound wave emitted by a speaker. The primary objective was to measure how the sound wave's intensity and amplitude vary in space and over time. We used a CNC-controlled scanning stage, a microphone, and MATLAB for data acquisition and analysis. The results help in understanding sound field behavior and validating theoretical models of wave propagation, offering insights into various engineering applications, including noise control and acoustic design.

The experimental setup involved using a CNC-controlled scanning stage that allowed precise movement of the microphone relative to the speaker. The speaker generated a 5 kHz tone, and the microphone captured the sound intensity at various positions. The scanning was automated using a “Rambo” Arduino system, which was controlled via MATLAB. The measurements were recorded across an array of positions to observe the acoustic wave’s behavior.

We used time-domain averaging to reduce random noise and enhance the signal clarity. Additionally, the time delay in the arrival of the sound wave at different positions was recorded. This time delay was used to estimate the speed of sound in air.

Conclusion

The results of this experiment validated theoretical models of sound wave propagation. While the experimental speed of sound was slightly lower than the theoretical value, this is likely due to environmental factors and measurement errors. The ability to visualize the sound field at various points in time using MATLAB allowed us to better understand wave behavior.

This experiment demonstrated the importance of systematic data collection and analysis in validating acoustic models, with applications in noise control and acoustics engineering.
